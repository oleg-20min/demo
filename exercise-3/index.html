01

More than a local dispute

The reporting in Cleared describes at least 33 eviction operations in Assam between 2021 and 2026, with more than 22,000 homes demolished and over 100,000 people displaced. What happened in Dhalpur in September 2021 was not the exception; it became the template.

That scale matters because it turns each bulldozer drive into part of a statewide system, not a one-off administrative act. The investigation centers that system before it narrows to the officials, court orders, and families inside it.

02

Evictions become a governing rhythm

The article draft marks 2025 as the busiest year in the dataset, with 11 documented operations and more than 40,000 people displaced. In the research file, Goalpara alone saw three large drives in June, July, and November 2025, while Darrang returned to the same Dhalpur-Sipajhar landscape that had already become synonymous with lethal force.

That rhythm is the story's first clue about intent. The pace suggests a program that is expanding, not a bureaucracy reluctantly enforcing isolated orders.

03

Who is behind it is not ambiguous

Finding `F9-C1` places Chief Minister Himanta Biswa Sarma at the center of the eviction campaign in his own words. On March 1, 2026, he stood before decorated bulldozers and promised that another 5 lakh bighas would be cleared after the election.

The research file treats that spectacle as more than rhetoric because it aligns with the broader arc in the article draft: evictions framed as a political message about who belongs in Assam and who can be removed from it.

04

The record of warning signs is extensive

Finding `F1-C1` says the Supreme Court's November 13, 2024 judgment formalized a nationwide demolition framework: prior notice, an opportunity to respond, and a written order. Finding `F2-C1` adds an Assam-specific framework on February 10, 2026 for forest evictions, including committee review and a mandatory 15-day notice period.

In Goalpara, the Gauhati High Court also issued targeted protective orders in July and August 2025, covering shelter, food, and the instruction that certain petitioners could not be evicted without leave of the court. By this point, the state could not plausibly claim the rules were unclear.

05

A pattern forms in the gap between law and enforcement

Finding `F5-C1` says the Supreme Court issued contempt notices to senior Assam officials over Goalpara demolitions that allegedly ignored the November 2024 guidelines, including a drive where residents received only two days notice. Finding `F11-C1` then connects the dots: legal protections kept arriving, but the demolitions kept resuming.

The state's counterargument appears in the same finding. Officials maintain that evictions from government land and reserve forest land sit outside at least some of those protections, creating the gray zone in which bulldozers keep moving.

06

Darrang remains the visual shorthand for the crisis

The article draft opens with the September 23, 2021 killings in Dhalpur, where police fired on a crowd and a government photographer was filmed stomping on a dying man. Finding `F6-C1` shows that the same Dhalpur-Sipajhar zone was targeted again on October 15, 2025 as part of the Garukhuti project, displacing another 167 families.

The research notes an important limit: it does not locate a Darrang-specific stay order, even though the broader pattern of court warnings is clear elsewhere. That gap matters, and the page keeps it visible rather than overstating what the files prove.

07

The bulldozer does not finish the job, it begins another crisis

The draft reports families living under tarpaulin and bamboo on roadsides, riverbanks, and forest edges after their homes were destroyed. Finding `F8-C1` confirms that by February 21, 2026, the Gauhati High Court was ordering the state to provide water, sanitation, medical care, and food grains to already-displaced families in Goalpara.

That sequence is the investigation's final argument. The state demolishes first, litigates later, and relief enters only after people have already been pushed into open-air survival.
